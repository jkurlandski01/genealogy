Sophia & Ludwig,

the Early Years

On this page we explore the early years of the marriage of Sophia and Ludwig Kurlandski, my great-grandparents on my father's father's side.

Before 1910

In 1901, Ludwig Kurlandsky left Poland via Antwerp and joined his brother in St. Louis. What happened next is, at this point, largely speculation. Probably he became part of the Polish immigrant community on the North Side. If he was like most Polish Catholic immigrants of the time, much of his social life would have centered around St. Stanislaus Church there.

St. Stanislaus entry in Wikipedia

We don't know when Ludwig met Sophia Wroblewski, but thanks to the 1910 census we can speculate that they married between 1903 (the year her last child from John Wroblewski was born) and 1907 (the year her first child with Ludwig was born). See the summary of the census below.

1910

The 1910 Census has the family living at 1920 Carr Street in St. Louis's Fourth Ward. By this time my great-grandfather had changed the spelling of both his first and his last name: Ludwig Kurlandsky, immigrant, became Ludwik Kurlanski, head of househould. We probably will never know how this happened, whether by accident or intention.

At this time he was living with two children of his own, and with two children by his wife's first marriage. The couple's eldest child is listed as Katie. So far in my genealogical quest, she is not part of the family's oral history. She would be listed as Katherine in the 1920 Census, and at that point--so far in my research--she disappears.

source: 1910 Census for Ludwik Kurlanski

I will soon have more on what I have learned from this census, but here's a quick summary:

  • Ludwig was a blacksmith, working at a wagon factory.
  • Their building had four flats in it, two in the front and two in the rear.
  • The couple was not living in an all-Polish ghetto. They had German and Irish neighbors.
  • The census-taker had trouble distinguishing between the language of Switzerland and the language of Sweden.
